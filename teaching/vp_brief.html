GUS — Graph Utility Scoring Framework

Technical Notes & Q&A  ·  VP Review
Research IJCAI 2026 Fraud Detection

What GUS Is

GUS (Graph Utility Scoring) is a decision framework that answers one specific engineering question before any model is built:

"Will a Graph Neural Network outperform XGBoost on this fraud dataset — and by how much?"

GUS gives a scored, data-driven answer in under an hour, using only the raw dataset. No GNN training required to get the answer.

Without GUS, teams build GNNs and discover later that XGBoost was already near-optimal. With GUS, that waste is caught at the start. The framework was validated across 12 real fraud datasets spanning 5 different domains.

The Problem GUS Solves

GNNs are expensive to build — new data pipeline, new model code, new debugging skills, weeks of engineering. XGBoost is fast, interpretable, and often already excellent. The question is always: is the GNN worth it?

Real example from our experiments: The Customs dataset had XGBoost at AUC = 0.9997. A GNN pushed it to 0.9998. That is a 0.0001 gain after weeks of work. GUS would have flagged this in under an hour and recommended shipping the XGBoost model.

The counterpoint: on the IP Blocklist dataset, XGBoost was at AUC 0.9116. The GNN improved it significantly. GUS correctly predicted this too. The framework tells you which situation you are in — before you build anything.

Step 1 — Feature Predictiveness

Train a simple Random Forest using only the raw tabular features — no graph at all. Measure AUC. This tells you how much fraud signal is already captured by the numbers alone.

Score rangeWhat it means
0.95 – 1.00Features almost perfectly predict fraud. A GNN has almost no room to help. Stop here, use XGBoost.
0.75 – 0.95Features are good but not perfect. A GNN may add value if homophily is also decent.
0.50 – 0.75Features are weak. The graph may supply the missing signal. GNN is more likely to win.

Step 2 — Fraud Homophily

Build a graph where transactions connected by a shared entity (same importer, same IP, same device) are linked. Then ask: what fraction of those edges connect two nodes that share the same label (both fraud, or both legitimate)?

Homophily = edges between same-label nodes / total edges > 0.5 → Fraud nodes tend to cluster together. GNN message-passing will amplify the fraud signal. < 0.5 → Fraud nodes are scattered randomly. GNN message-passing will average away the signal.

Step 3 — XGBoost Baseline AUC

Train XGBoost on the same data. Record AUC. This is the baseline that the GNN must beat to be worth building. Critically, this also tells us how much "room" is left for any improvement.

Step 4 — Effective Graph Utility (EGU)

This is the core GUS score. It combines graph structure quality with remaining improvement room:

Effective_GU = Graph_Utility × (1 − XGBoost_AUC) Where: Graph_Utility = how much the graph structure adds (from homophily + empirical GNN runs) (1 − XGB_AUC) = room for improvement (if XGB=0.9997, room = 0.0003 — almost nothing)
Why this formula works: A dataset can have a highly useful graph structure (high Graph_Utility) but still not benefit from a GNN, if XGBoost has already captured almost all the predictive signal (XGB_AUC near 1.0). EGU catches both dimensions at once.

Step 5 — The Decision Rule

IF Effective_GU > 0.01 → BUILD THE GNN (TH-GFD recommended) ELSE → SHIP XGBOOST (GNN will not justify the cost)

This threshold (0.01) was determined empirically and validated across 12 datasets. The decision rule achieves 75% accuracy in predicting which model wins. Where it is uncertain (borderline cases), the two models are so close in performance that the choice rarely matters practically.

Q&A

Q1 — What exactly is GUS in one sentence?
GUS is a scoring method that predicts — before you build a GNN — whether a Graph Neural Network will meaningfully outperform XGBoost on a given fraud detection dataset, using two measurable properties of the data: how well features alone predict fraud, and how strongly fraud nodes cluster together in the graph.
Q2 — Why does 75% accuracy feel low for a decision framework?
The three "Mixed" cases (Twitter Bots, Yelp, Fake Job) are genuinely borderline — the GNN and XGBoost perform nearly identically on them, so there is no clearly correct answer. On all nine datasets where one model clearly wins, GUS is correct every single time. The 75% figure includes those three ties. In practice, if GUS says "toss-up," the team can default to XGBoost (faster and cheaper) with minimal expected cost.
Q3 — Why does EGU multiply by (1 − XGB_AUC)?
Because graph utility is only useful if there is improvement left to capture. The Customs dataset has a meaningful Graph_Utility score (0.11), but XGBoost already sits at AUC 0.9997. The remaining gap is 0.0003. No model — GNN or otherwise — can move the needle meaningfully in that gap. Multiplying by (1 − XGB_AUC) collapses EGU to near zero in this case, correctly blocking a GNN recommendation.
Q4 — How is this different from just running both models and comparing?
Running both models costs weeks (GNN training is expensive and iterative). GUS gives you the answer in under an hour using only a Random Forest on the raw features plus a graph construction and homophily calculation. The GNN itself is never trained during the GUS screening phase. The empirical validation on 12 datasets gives us confidence the EGU score is reliable enough to make the decision without running the GNN.
Q5 — What is the insight behind Fraud Homophily mattering?
GNNs learn by averaging information from neighbours. If a fraud node's neighbours are mostly also fraud, that averaging amplifies the fraud signal. If a fraud node's neighbours are a random mix of fraud and legitimate, that averaging washes out the signal — the GNN actually makes predictions worse. Homophily tells you which situation you are in. This is why Malicious URLs (homophily = 0.756) still gets an XGBoost recommendation — because the features are so strong (0.970) that there is nothing left for even a well-structured graph to add.
Q6 — What is the GUS AUC prediction formula and when would we use it?
From a linear regression fit across the 12 datasets:

Predicted_AUC ≈ 0.611 × Feature_Pred − 0.038 × Fraud_Homo + 0.076 × Fraud_Rate + 0.398 (R² = 0.61)

Use case: when a new dataset arrives and you want an instant rough estimate of baseline model performance before running any experiments. Feature Predictiveness alone (coefficient 0.611) is the dominant term — if you have that number, you already have a reasonable AUC estimate.
Q7 — Is GUS specific to fraud detection or more general?
The framework is general — it applies to any node classification problem where the question is "GNN vs. tabular model." The specific metrics (Revenue@K, customs-specific entities) are fraud-domain details, but EGU = Graph_Utility × (1 − Baseline_AUC) and the homophily calculation work on any graph classification problem. The 0.01 threshold was calibrated on fraud data and would need revalidation on a different domain.
Q8 — What comes after GUS says "build the GNN"?
GUS hands off to TH-GFD — the actual GNN model in this framework. TH-GFD builds a heterogeneous graph from the transaction entities, applies temporal-weighted attention during message passing, and trains in two phases (self-supervised pre-training first, then semi-supervised fine-tuning). TH-GFD is not part of the GUS decision — it is what GUS recommends deploying when the decision is "GNN."
Q9 — What are the next steps to make GUS stronger?
Three priorities: (1) Add more datasets to push decision accuracy above 75%, especially examples that stress-test the borderline region. (2) Investigate why Twitter Bots and Yelp sit on the boundary — understand what structural property differentiates them from clear GNN wins. (3) Refine the EGU threshold of 0.01 — test whether a slightly different threshold or a two-threshold "uncertain zone" performs better.
Q10 — How is GUS validated statistically?
Two ways. First, Pearson correlation between EGU scores and actual GNN improvement over XGBoost across 12 datasets: r = 0.6434, p = 0.024. This confirms EGU is a statistically significant predictor at the 5% level — it is not random noise. Second, the decision rule (EGU > 0.01 → GNN) was evaluated as a binary classifier across the same 12 datasets: 75% accuracy, with all errors on genuinely borderline cases.
